# Buyer Context
## Who Is Across the Table

The person across the table is rarely one person. 
It's a buying committee. Speak all three languages in the same 
meeting without losing the thread.

---

## The Business Owner

**Thinks in:** Customers, margins, speed to value, team adoption.  
**Moves:** Fast. Will be your champion or your loudest critic.  
**Fears:** Customer-facing failure. Team adoption failure. 
Being the visible face of a pilot that goes wrong.  
**Asks:** "Will my team actually use this? How fast can we be productive? 
What happens when something doesn't work in front of a customer?"

**Speak their language:** Workflow outcomes, time-to-value, 
concrete use cases, honest ramp time expectations.

---

## The Business Leader

**Thinks in:** ROI, risk framing, board-level narrative.  
**Moves:** Slowly. Signs the check. Must defend the decision 
upward without preparation.  
**Fears:** A headline with their company name and the word "AI" 
next to something bad. Signing off on a tool their team can't use.  
**Asks:** "What's the ROI? What's the risk if this goes wrong? 
How do I explain this investment to my board?"

**Speak their language:** Cost reduction, competitive positioning, 
risk mitigation, clear accountability.

**Does not care about:** Voice quality metrics. Slider settings. 
API latency numbers.

---

## The IT Administrator

**Thinks in:** Uptime, security posture, approved vendor lists, 
data flow documentation.  
**Moves:** Quietly. Can kill the deal after everyone else has said yes.  
**Fears:** A security incident that traces back to a system they approved. 
Being asked questions they can't answer.  
**Asks:** "Where does our data go? Who can access our voice clones? 
What's the data retention policy? Is this on our approved vendor list?"

**Speak their language:** Data flows, compliance posture, 
security documentation, integration architecture.

---

## Secondary Personas — Enterprise Deals

At Business tier, two additional stakeholders frequently appear. 
They are not always in the room, but their questions arrive through 
the primary personas — or in writing, after the meeting ends.

### Legal Counsel

**Thinks in:** Liability, IP ownership, contract scope, indemnification.  
**Moves:** Slowly and in writing. Rarely on the call; always reviewing the contract.  
**Fears:** The company using a voice it doesn't have rights to. 
A licensing violation that becomes a legal event.  
**Asks:** "Who owns the cloned voice asset? What are the use case 
restrictions on Library voices? What happens if generated audio is 
used outside the licensed scope?"

**Speak their language:** Rights ownership, license scope, 
indemnification, documented consent workflow.

### Finance

**Thinks in:** Budget lines, overage exposure, forecasting, cost models.  
**Moves:** Methodically. Wants a number they can defend in a budget meeting.  
**Fears:** A surprise invoice that makes the tool look uncontrolled. 
Being asked to explain a cost overrun they didn't see coming.  
**Asks:** "What's the worst-case monthly cost? How do overages work? 
Can we cap consumption?"

**Speak their language:** Predictable cost models, consumption 
forecasting, overage structure, upgrade thresholds.

---

## The CSM's Job

Speak all three languages in the same meeting without losing the thread.  
Never let the IT Administrator's question land without an answer.  
Never let the Business Leader sit through a technical explanation 
that doesn't connect back to ROI.  
Never let the Business Owner's urgency steamroll the IT Administrator's concerns.

*A CSM who surfaces the hard questions proactively builds trust with all three. 
A CSM who waits for buyers to discover problems on their own creates churn events.*
