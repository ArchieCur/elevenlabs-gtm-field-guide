# Module 6: The Buying Committee Playbook
## How to Hold the Room When Everyone Speaks a Different Language

---

### The Ambush Risk

The buying committee meeting doesn't fail because the CSM
doesn't know the product.

It fails because the CSM loses the room — to a monologue,
to a rabbit hole question, to one stakeholder's agenda
crowding out the other two. By the time the meeting ends,
the Business Owner is excited, the IT Administrator has
three unanswered questions, and the Business Leader has
mentally moved on to their next calendar item.

Nobody said no. Nobody said yes. The deal stalls.

The CSM who controls the room isn't the loudest voice
or the most technically knowledgeable. They're the one
who remembers that every person in that room gave up
something to be there — and that respect for their time
is the only consensus that already exists before
the first slide is shown.

---

### Who Is in the Room

Before walking in, know which of these personas are present
and what each one needs to leave with.

**The Business Owner** needs to believe the platform
solves their specific problem and that their team
can actually use it. They move fast and lose patience
when the conversation goes technical without payoff.

**The Business Leader** needs something they can
defend upward — ROI language, risk framing,
a decision they can explain to someone who wasn't
in the room. They tune out when the conversation
gets operational.

**The IT Administrator** needs to know the platform
won't create problems for them after the contract
is signed. They go quiet when their questions
aren't answered — and that quiet is not agreement.
*For the specific questions IT Administrators ask —
and the answers to have ready — see Module 8:
Data Security and Compliance.*

**Legal and Finance** (when present) need a specific
thing each: Legal needs to know what's covered
and what isn't. Finance needs to know what the
worst-case invoice looks like.

*For full persona profiles, see buyer-context.md.*

### The Three Ways the Room Goes Wrong

---

#### Problem 1: The Performance Stakeholder

**What happens:** A stakeholder — usually the most senior
person in the room or the one with the most technical
knowledge — delivers a monologue. No question is attached.
The content is accurate but the purpose is display:
they are demonstrating expertise in front of their peers.

The CSM who waits for a question that isn't coming
loses two minutes and the attention of everyone else
in the room.

**What the performance stakeholder wants:**
Recognition. Not more work.

**The redirect:**
> *"I see. You really seem to be invested in making
> this work — and I'm sure the other stakeholders
> will come to rely on your expertise as this rolls out."*

This sentence does three things simultaneously:
it validates them, it acknowledges their knowledge,
and it subtly reframes their expertise as a future
responsibility rather than a current floor.
The monologue stops. The room moves.

**What not to do:** Do not ask them a follow-up question.
A follow-up question is an invitation to continue.
Validate and redirect — then immediately bring
another stakeholder into the conversation.

**The pivot:** Immediately address another persona by name
with a direct, specific question:
> *"[Other name] — from your team's perspective, how does
> that align with what you're currently working with?"*

The named question serves two purposes: it gives the
performance stakeholder a graceful exit (someone else
is now speaking), and it re-centers a persona whose
attention may have drifted.

---

#### Problem 2: The Rabbit Hole Question

**What happens:** A stakeholder asks a question that is
legitimate but too specific for the group. The question
is real — it deserves an answer — but answering it
fully in this room serves only the person who asked
and loses everyone else.

This is not the same as a question the CSM can't answer.
It's a question the CSM could answer at length —
but shouldn't, here, now.

**The two-part technique:**

*Step 1: Neutral acknowledgment while they speak.*
Do not fill the air. Listen visibly.
> *"I see." / "Mm-hmm." / "That's helpful context."*

These phrases signal engagement without encouraging
expansion. They are not agreement. They are not dismissal.
They buy the moment needed for Step 2.

*Step 2: At the breath — park and bridge.*
> *"[Name] brings up a good point — that's a real issue
> and I want to make sure we address it properly.
> Could we hold that for a moment? Because what
> [Name] is describing may actually connect to
> something that affects everyone in the room."*

Then weave one word or phrase from their question
into the next point made to the group.

**Why weaving works:** It signals to the stakeholder
that they were genuinely heard — which is often
what they needed more than the answer. It also
demonstrates to the rest of the room that the CSM
listens and connects, rather than deflects.

**After the meeting:** Follow up on the parked question
directly and specifically. A parked question that
is never answered becomes a churn signal.

---

#### Problem 3: The Accidental Authority

**What happens:** A stakeholder — often someone who has
done research, used a competitor product, or has
strong technical opinions — begins speaking about
ElevenLabs with enough confidence that other
stakeholders start deferring to them instead of
to the CSM.

This is one of the trickiest room control problems
because the stakeholder is often not wrong —
they may have accurate information. But a stakeholder
who becomes the product authority in their own
buying committee has shifted the narrative
outside the CSM's control.

**How to recognize it:** Other stakeholders start
directing questions to the knowledgeable stakeholder
instead of to the CSM. The knowledgeable stakeholder
starts answering before the CSM can.

**The reclaim — without contradiction:**
> *"That's exactly right — and the way we've seen
> that work in practice for teams at this scale is...
> [expand, add specificity, redirect to CSM position]."*

This move confirms their accuracy, adds a layer
of specificity they can't match (practitioner
knowledge vs. research knowledge), and reestablishes
the CSM as the source of ground-truth information.

**What not to do:** Do not contradict the accidental
authority directly in front of their peers.
Even if they are wrong, a public correction
creates an adversary. Confirm what's correct,
add what's missing, move forward.

### Holding All Three Personas Simultaneously

The fundamental challenge of the buying committee meeting
is that all three primary personas are present at once —
and a point that lands for one can lose the other two.

**The rule:** No single persona should own more than
two consecutive minutes of the conversation.

When the conversation goes technical (IT Administrator
territory), surface the business outcome immediately after:
> *"What that means for your team practically is..."*

When the conversation goes operational (Business Owner
territory), surface the strategic frame immediately after:
> *"From a business risk perspective, what this protects is..."*

When the conversation goes strategic (Business Leader
territory), surface the concrete next step immediately after:
> *"The way teams typically get started with this is..."*

Each pivot keeps the persona who was just served
engaged while re-entering the personas who were
starting to drift.

### The Fine-Tuning Conversation as a Room Control Example

The voice fine-tuning learning curve (Module 4) is one of
the most useful real-world examples of how the same
topic lands differently with each persona.

**The Business Owner** hears: "My team is going to
struggle with this and it will slow us down."
Address this first. Acknowledge the ramp time,
name the onboarding support that prevents it.

**The Business Leader** hears: "This is a productivity
risk I didn't budget for."
Address this with the ramp timeline and the
week-one onboarding investment that protects ROI.

**The IT Administrator** hears: "Settings that don't
save per project is a support ticket waiting to happen."
Address this with the external documentation workaround
and the golden reference recording protocol.

Same topic. Three different concerns. Three different
answers that need to land in the same conversation.

The CSM who prepares all three answers before walking
in doesn't get ambushed by any of them.

### When a Limitation Comes Up in the Room

When any stakeholder surfaces a platform limitation
in a buying committee meeting, the Exit framework
applies — but the room context changes the stakes.

A limitation acknowledged gracefully in front of
three stakeholders simultaneously either builds
trust with all three or loses it with all three.

*For the full Exit framework (Exit 0 through Exit 3),
see Module 7: The Honest Limitations Card.*

The buying committee adaptation: use Exit 0 (The Show Me)
before any other exit whenever possible. Inviting the
stakeholder who raised the limitation to walk the room
through their specific use case shifts the dynamic
from "CSM defending a weakness" to "CSM facilitating
a problem-solving conversation." That's the move.

### Closing the Room

The buying committee meeting ends well when every
persona leaves with something specific:

- **Business Owner:** A clear next step they own
- **Business Leader:** A number or outcome they
  can repeat to someone who wasn't in the room
- **IT Administrator:** A confirmed path to
  get their open questions answered
- **Legal / Finance (if present):** A named
  follow-up that addresses their specific concern

If any persona leaves without their specific thing,
the deal doesn't advance — it floats.

**The closing move:**
> *"Before we wrap — I want to make sure I've addressed
> what matters most to each of you. [Name], for your
> team's workflow, the next step is X. [Name], on the
> ROI question, I'll follow up with Y. [Name], for
> the technical questions, let's schedule Z."*

Naming each persona's next step in the room —
in front of the others — creates a shared
record of commitments. It also signals to the
Business Leader that the CSM runs a tight process.
That signal matters more than it seems.

---

### The Only Pre-Existing Consensus

Every person in that buying committee gave up time
from their actual job to be there. The Business Owner
moved a team standup. The Business Leader compressed
their afternoon. The IT Administrator paused a
support queue.

That shared sacrifice — before a single word
about ElevenLabs is spoken — is the only thing
every persona in the room already agrees on.

The CSM who honors that by being prepared, moving
efficiently, keeping the room from being hijacked,
and ensuring every persona leaves with something
concrete has already demonstrated something
more important than product knowledge:

They've demonstrated that working with ElevenLabs
looks like this.

That is the close that doesn't need to be said aloud.
