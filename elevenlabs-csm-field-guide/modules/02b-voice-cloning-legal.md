# Module 2B: Voice Cloning — Consent, IP, and Legal Workflow
## What Has to Be True Before Any Cloning Begins

---

### The Ambush Risk

Voice cloning is the capability in the ElevenLabs platform
that carries the most legal complexity. Not the most technical
complexity — the most legal complexity.

A CSM who answers a consent or IP question confidently
and incorrectly has created a legal problem for the customer
and a liability problem for ElevenLabs. A CSM who says
*"that's exactly the kind of question our team needs
to be part of"* has created a relationship.

This module arms the CSM to have the legal conversation
credibly — not to answer it definitively. Every section
that approaches legal territory ends with an escalation
path, not a conclusion. That is by design.

---

### Why This Conversation Happens Before Cloning Begins

The legal questions in a voice cloning project don't surface
after the clone is created. They surface after the clone
is *used* — in a campaign that didn't clear the rights,
in content the voice talent didn't approve, in a market
where the consent documentation doesn't meet local requirements.

By that point, the clone exists, the content has been
distributed, and the question is no longer hypothetical.

The CSM who has the legal conversation before the first
sample is submitted is the CSM whose customer doesn't
call with a crisis six weeks after contract signing.

### Three Legal Conversations in Every Cloning Project

---

### Conversation 1: Consent — Who Agreed to This?

**[Legal] [Business Owner]**

ElevenLabs requires that customers have obtained proper
consent from the voice talent before submitting audio
for cloning. This is not optional and it is not a
formality — it is a Terms of Service requirement.

**What consent means at the platform level:**
The person whose voice is being cloned must have
agreed to the cloning. That agreement needs to be
documented — not assumed, not implied, not inherited
from a prior employment or talent agreement that
predates AI voice cloning as a technology.

**The three consent scenarios a CSM will encounter:**

*Scenario A: The customer is cloning their own voice.*
Simplest case. The customer is the voice talent.
Consent is self-evident — but the documentation
still matters. ElevenLabs' Terms of Service apply.
The customer should confirm they understand
what they are agreeing to.

*Scenario B: The customer is cloning an employee's voice.*
More complex. An employment agreement does not
automatically grant consent for voice cloning.
The employee's voice is their biometric data. Consent
requirements for biometric data collection vary by
jurisdiction and may extend beyond what a standard
employment agreement covers. Your legal team can
confirm what applies to your specific situation
before you proceed.

**CSM talking point:**
> "Has your legal team confirmed that your standard
> employment agreements cover voice cloning specifically?
> This is an area where what worked for video or photo
> release may not be sufficient. It's worth a quick
> check before we proceed."

*Scenario C: The customer is cloning external voice talent.*
Most complex. Requires explicit written consent from
the talent, clarity on what the consent covers
(which uses, which markets, for how long), and
confirmation that the talent's existing representation
agreements don't restrict AI voice licensing.

**CSM talking point:**
> "For external talent, we'll want to make sure
> the consent documentation covers the specific
> use case — not just 'AI voice cloning' generally.
> If the talent has representation, their agent
> or manager may need to be part of that conversation."

**What the CSM does not answer:**
Whether specific consent language is legally sufficient
in any jurisdiction. Whether an existing talent agreement
covers voice cloning. Whether biometric consent laws
apply to a specific customer's situation.
These are legal counsel questions, not CSM questions.

---

### Conversation 2: IP Ownership — Who Owns the Clone?

**[Legal] [Business Owner] [Business Leader]**

The practical ownership question that every Business Owner
and Business Leader will ask: *"If we create a voice clone,
who owns it?"*

**At the platform level, the answer is reasonably clear:**
The customer owns the voice clone they create from audio
they have the rights to use. ElevenLabs does not claim
ownership of customer-created voice clones.

**Where ownership gets complicated:**

*The underlying rights question:*
Owning the clone requires having the rights to the
source audio. If the source audio is a recording
of a third party whose consent was not properly
obtained, the ownership of the clone is disputed —
regardless of who created it technically.

*The derivative works question:*
Some voice talent agreements include provisions
about derivative works — works created from
a performance. Whether a voice clone constitutes
a derivative work is an unsettled legal question
in many jurisdictions. Legal counsel, not a CSM,
is the right person to assess this for a specific
talent agreement.

*The employment works question:*
If an employee's voice is cloned in the course
of their employment, questions about who owns
that voice asset — the employee or the employer —
may not be resolved by the employment agreement alone.

**CSM talking point:**
> "At the platform level, you own the clone you create.
> The fuller ownership picture — especially for external
> talent or employee voices — depends on the agreements
> you have in place. That's a conversation worth having
> with your legal team before we scale the use of
> any cloned voice in production."

**What the CSM does not answer:**
Whether a specific talent agreement transfers cloning rights.
Whether a voice clone constitutes a derivative work.
Whether an employer owns a voice clone of an employee.
These are legal counsel questions.

### Conversation 3: Liability — What If Something Goes Wrong?

**[Legal] [Finance] [Business Leader]**

This is the conversation the CSM should be least
willing to have in detail — and most willing to
surface and escalate.

**The scenarios that generate liability questions:**

*Misuse of a cloned voice:*
A voice clone used for content the talent didn't
approve — different markets, different use cases,
different messages than the consent covered.

*Unauthorized distribution:*
A cloned voice used in ways that exceed the
license or consent scope — broadcasted, sublicensed,
or used by a third party the talent didn't agree to.

*Deepfake risk:*
A cloned voice used to generate content that
misrepresents what the person said or implies
endorsement they didn't give.

*Data breach or unauthorized access:*
Voice training data accessed or used outside
the agreed scope.

**The CSM's role in this conversation:**
Name the scenarios. Confirm they are real risks.
Exit to escalation.

**CSM talking point:**
> "These are the scenarios where having the right
> agreements in place before you start protects you.
> The specifics — what your liability exposure looks
> like, what your agreements need to say — are
> exactly what our enterprise team and your legal
> counsel should work through together.
> Can we get those conversations scheduled
> before production begins?"

**What the CSM does not answer:**
What the customer's liability exposure is in any scenario.
Whether their current agreements are sufficient.
What constitutes misuse under applicable law.
These are legal counsel questions, not CSM questions.

### ElevenLabs Terms of Service — What the CSM Should Know

**[Legal]**

The CSM does not need to have the ElevenLabs Terms of Service
memorized. The CSM needs to know three things:

**1. Consent is a ToS requirement, not a courtesy.**
Cloning a voice without the subject's consent violates
ElevenLabs' Prohibited Use Policy — a binding supplement
to the Terms of Service, last updated September 2025.
Section 5 of that policy is explicit on this point.
A customer who proceeds without proper consent is
not just taking a legal risk — they are in breach
of their agreement with ElevenLabs.

CSMs should direct customers to the current published
policy rather than quoting it from memory — the policy
is updated periodically and the published version is
the authoritative one.

**2. Prohibited use cases exist.**
ElevenLabs' ToS prohibits specific use cases for
cloned voices — including content designed to deceive,
defame, or misrepresent. The CSM who is asked
"can we use this for X" where X is near any of
these boundaries should not answer. They should
escalate to ElevenLabs' team for a ToS review.

**3. The ToS evolves.**
Voice cloning law is moving faster than most
technology law. ElevenLabs' Terms of Service
are updated to reflect new legal requirements
and platform policies. The CSM should not quote
specific ToS language from memory — they should
direct the customer to the current published
Terms of Service and involve ElevenLabs' team
for any detailed interpretation.

### Voice Training Data — Where It Lives and Who Controls It

**[Legal] [IT Administrator]**

For customers with data residency, privacy, or compliance
requirements, the voice training data question will come up:

- Where is the audio submitted for cloning stored?
- Who at ElevenLabs can access it?
- What is the retention and deletion policy?
- Does it leave ElevenLabs' infrastructure?

**The CSM's role:** Surface these questions.
Confirm they are legitimate and important.
Route them to Module 8 (Data Security and Compliance)
for the framework, and to ElevenLabs' technical
and legal team for the specific answers.

*See Module 8: Data Security and Compliance
for the full data handling framework.*

The CSM does not answer data residency or
retention questions from memory.

### Before Any Cloning Begins — The Legal Checklist

*This legal checklist pairs with Module 2A's technical
checklist. Both must be complete before any audio
is submitted for cloning.*

Before any customer submits audio for cloning —
Instant or Professional — the CSM should confirm:

☐ **Consent confirmed:** The voice talent has given
   explicit consent for voice cloning for this
   specific use case. Consent is documented.

☐ **Agreement reviewed:** The customer's legal team
   has reviewed relevant talent agreements for
   AI voice cloning provisions.

☐ **Use case scoped:** The intended uses of the
   cloned voice are defined — which markets,
   which content types, which distribution channels.
   The consent covers the intended scope.

☐ **Prohibited uses acknowledged:** The customer
   understands ElevenLabs' ToS restrictions
   on cloned voice use.

☐ **Data handling confirmed:** For Business tier
   customers with compliance requirements,
   data residency and retention questions have
   been routed to ElevenLabs' technical team.

☐ **Legal counsel engaged:** For any external talent
   cloning, employee voice cloning, or use cases
   near ToS boundaries, the customer's legal
   counsel has been involved before submission.

**If any item is unchecked:** Do not proceed.
Route the open item to the appropriate resource —
ElevenLabs' enterprise team, the customer's legal
counsel, or both — before cloning begins.

### The Escalation Path

When any legal question in a cloning project
exceeds the CSM's knowledge domain:

**Step 1:** Name the question clearly.
> *"That's an important question about [consent /
> ownership / liability] — and it's one that
> needs more than my answer."*

**Step 2:** Confirm it's real and worth pursuing.
> *"This is exactly the kind of thing that protects
> you downstream — getting it right now is much
> easier than addressing it after production begins."*

**Step 3:** Route to the right resource.
> *"For questions about compliance documentation, data handling
> agreements, and regulatory requirements, ElevenLabs maintains
> a Trust Center at compliance.elevenlabs.io with legal and
> compliance documents available to enterprise customers.
> Some documents require access requests — your ElevenLabs
> account contact can facilitate that process.
> For questions about your organization's own legal obligations
> around consent and IP, that conversation belongs with
> your legal counsel. Both tracks matter and both should
> happen before production begins."*

The CSM who executes this three-step exit consistently
is the CSM whose customers feel protected, not deflected.
There is a meaningful difference between "I don't know"
and "this deserves a more expert answer than I can give
you — and here's how to get it."
